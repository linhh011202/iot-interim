## 1. Introduction and Research Objectives

### 1.1. Background

Digital Cultural Heritage Resources (DCHR) constitute a digital resource system formed through the digitization of tangible cultural heritage, including artifacts and architectural monuments. These resources play a critical role in cultural heritage preservation, transmission, and dissemination. Stored digitally in cyberspace, DCHR effectively mitigates threats from natural disasters (earthquakes, floods, typhoons) and human-induced damages (armed conflicts). By digitizing cultural artifacts, these resources ensure that even if physical items are lost or damaged, their digital counterparts remain intact for future generations.

Among emerging digital technologies, **blockchain** stands out for its transformative potential in the management and conservation of cultural heritage, with its core attributes of decentralization and immutability offering a secure and transparent platform for documenting, owning, and transferring cultural assets.

### 1.2. Primary Applications of Blockchain in Cultural Heritage

1. **Data integrity and authenticity certification**: Providing immutable historical records of artifacts, enhancing authenticity assessments and preventing forgery.
2. **Tokenization of cultural assets**: Through Non-Fungible Tokens (NFTs), enabling fractional ownership and decentralized stewardship of cultural relics.
3. **Digital rights management**: Protecting intellectual property associated with digital manifestations of cultural heritage.
4. **Distributed consensus**: Validating the authenticity of cultural relics, safeguarding against forgery and unauthorized transactions.

### 1.3. Research Objectives

The study investigates:
- The application potential of blockchain in DCHR management
- Technical implementation pathways
- Future development trends and key research directions

### 1.4. Multidimensional Impact

- **Economic**: Construction of blockchain and digital-twin-driven assetization platforms, NFT-based ownership authentication, smart-contract-enabled automated revenue reinvestment into conservation initiatives (a "conservation-value-added-reconservation" closed-loop system).
- **Technological**: Integration of blockchain, AI-driven restoration algorithms, and cross-chain protocols; promotion of international standardization of metadata and smart contract frameworks.
- **Policymaking**: Design of cross-border data circulation and tax incentive frameworks; blockchain-enabled multinational collaborative governance for copyright authentication and illicit circulation tracking.
- **Educational**: Development of globally accessible high-precision digital resource repositories and VR/DAO-based immersive tools.

## 2. Blockchain Technology Overview

### 2.1. Blockchain Evolution

The paper traces blockchain's evolution through four stages:

| Stage | Period | Key Features |
|---|---|---|
| **Blockchain 1.0** | 2008+ | Cryptocurrencies and payment systems (Bitcoin, Satoshi Nakamoto) |
| **Blockchain 2.0** | ~2014+ | Smart contracts and platforms (Ethereum); expanded financial sector applications |
| **Blockchain 3.0** | ~2017+ | Beyond finance: supply chain management, identity verification |
| **Blockchain 4.0** | Current | Integration of AI, IoT; advanced features |

In China, despite a contraction in industry size to approximately 6 billion RMB by 2023, blockchain-related enterprises continue to rise, and national blockchain standards have been introduced.

### 2.2. Core Blockchain Features

The paper highlights four fundamental properties:

1. **Immutability**: Each block is cryptographically linked to the preceding one through hash functions; altering a block invalidates all subsequent hashes and is detected by network nodes. This property ensures reliable record storage and verification.

2. **Decentralization**: Data is distributed across multiple nodes, eliminating central control points. This enhances network resilience and attack resistance. In public blockchains, all nodes have transaction verification and consensus participation rights; in private/consortium chains, this extends to authorized entities (e.g., cultural heritage institutions, regulatory agencies).

3. **Transparency**: All transaction records are visible to network participants, fostering trust and accountability. Encryption can be used to protect user privacy while maintaining transparency.

4. **Security**: Alteration of any block is detected via consensus mechanism and rejected by other nodes. Tampering would require altering the majority of nodes, which is practically infeasible.

### 2.3. Classification of Blockchain

| Type | Characteristics | DCHR Example |
|---|---|---|
| **Public Blockchain** | Decentralized and transparent; anyone can participate in transactions and consensus | "History_Memory_and_Identity" NFT collection: Ukrainian cultural heritage sites transformed into digital assets with on-chain smart contracts + off-chain storage (IPFS/Arweave) |
| **Private Blockchain** | Write permissions restricted to internal entities; read access configurable | Byzantine Fresco case (Church of Cyprus and Byzantine Fresco Foundation): stringent control over critical data integrity; write permissions confined to ownership entities, museums, and conservation institutions |
| **Consortium Blockchain** | Pre-selected authorized nodes jointly manage records; balances control and collaborative openness | Florence Museum Consortium: distributed ledger for artwork loans; UNESCO intangible heritage projects restricting node participation to authenticated cultural institutions |

The paper notes that consortium blockchains offer a "semi-open architecture" that preserves stakeholder control while achieving cross-chain data interoperability through standardized interfaces.

## 3. Digital Cultural Heritage Resources (DCHR)

### 3.1. DCHR Ontological Taxonomy

The paper categorizes DCHR into two major types: **high-fidelity digital replicas** and **metadata-driven digital resources**. A detailed taxonomy is provided:

| Category | Definition |
|---|---|
| **Ontological digital resources** | Data directly representing visual and structural attributes of cultural artifacts (appearance, shape, extent of physical damage) |
| **Descriptive digital resources** | Data capturing physical characteristics (name, age, material composition, size, identifying properties) |
| **Interpretative digital resources** | Data facilitating scientific, cultural, and artistic interpretation (archeological, historical, and contextual significance) |
| **Derived digital resources** | Data reconstructed or recontextualized from descriptive and interpretative resources (digital collections, virtual reconstructions, cultural games) |
| **Management digital resources** | Data produced by cultural institutions during administration (cataloging identifiers, locations, acquisition dates, storage details) |

### 3.2. DCHR Digitization Phases

The paper describes a three-phase framework:

1. **Preventive digitization**: High-precision scanning, photography, and 3D modeling to capture cultural relics and convert them into digital formats.
2. **Crisis-responsive technologies**: Digital preservation during emergencies and conflicts.
3. **Interactive DCHR**: Enabling global accessibility and resilience against threats.

### 3.3. Challenges Facing DCHR

The primary challenges fall into two domains:

**Data Security:**
- **Data authentication**: Verifying the authenticity and provenance of digital cultural relics. Forgeries or misattribution undermine reliability.
- **Privacy protection**: Balancing blockchain transparency with privacy for sensitive cultural heritage information. The immutable nature of blockchain necessitates stringent controls at data input.

**Resource Sharing and Utilization:**
- High costs and technical demands of digitization
- Large volume of digital data requiring extensive storage, backup, and migration
- Clarification of copyright and intellectual property rights
- Equitable resource sharing and prevention of misuse

**Solutions proposed:**
- Blockchain's decentralized, irreversible, and transparent features for data authentication
- Cryptographic-PUF (Physical Unclonable Function) architectures for data provenance assurance
- Blockchain-based metadata encapsulation for automated IP management via smart contracts
- Advanced encryption techniques, refined access control mechanisms, and standardized input data formats
- Systems like **Salsal** and **MApp** demonstrating notable achievements in these areas

## 4. Consensus Mechanisms

### 4.1. Definition

Blockchain's core features of decentralization and cryptographic chaining enable distributed nodes to achieve consensus without central authority, characterized by three properties:
- **Security**: Ensures data integrity by preventing tampering
- **Liveness**: Incentivizes participation and maintains continuous network operation
- **Fault tolerance**: Ensures consensus despite disruptions

### 4.2. Classification and Analysis for DCHR

The paper provides a detailed analysis of each consensus mechanism's suitability for DCHR:

| Mechanism | Key Features | Suitability for DCHR |
|---|---|---|
| **Proof-of-Work (PoW)** | Computational puzzles for validation; robust security and decentralization; high energy consumption, limited scalability | **Incompatible** with DCHR due to structural security deficiencies and disproportionately high energy expenditure |
| **Proof-of-Stake (PoS)** | Validators selected by crypto holdings; improved energy efficiency and transaction speed; "nothing-at-stake" vulnerability | **Partially viable** for DCHR systems incorporating NFT transaction modules; centralizes authority among resource-endowed nodes, violating equitable participation |
| **Proof-of-Authority (PoA)** | Block creation based on validator identity/reputation; operational efficiency, low energy | **Incompatible** with permissionless, egalitarian DCHR; viable for centralized DCHR requiring trusted validators |
| **BFT** | Consensus through voting/filtering; tolerates up to 1/3 malicious nodes; performance overhead | **Applicable** to DCHR systems, though efficiency considers necessitate alternatives |
| **Raft** | High-efficiency via leader election, log replication, and safety guarantees; three-phase mechanism | **Most suitable** for DCHR systems requiring centralized administration; improved security over PoA through fault-tolerant leader election |

### 4.3. Enhanced Raft Algorithm for DCHR

The paper proposes an enhanced Raft algorithm as the most suitable consensus mechanism for DCHR, offering:
- Tri-phase mechanism: leader election, log replication, safety guarantees
- Leader election via majority voting among nodes (triggered by heartbeat timeouts)
- Sequential log entries broadcast for synchronization with strong consistency
- Safety assurance through log index verification, term constraints, and fault tolerance
- Significantly improved system security compared to PoA, with fault-tolerant leader election preventing complete system breakdown from single node failure
- **Limitation**: If predefined nodes suffer sustained attacks, they cannot recover privileges, conflicting with scenarios requiring stable centralized governance

### 4.4. Functioning of Consensus Mechanisms in DCHR

The paper emphasizes that consensus mechanisms:
- Play a pivotal role in transaction verification and ensuring irreversibility
- Mitigate fraudulent activities such as double-spending
- Work synergistically with smart contracts to improve operational efficiency
- Must be selected according to specific application scenarios (e.g., sharding for high-concurrency; BFT for low-trust environments)

## 5. Blockchain Implementation in DCHR

### 5.1. Case Study: Digital Dunhuang Project

The Digital Dunhuang Open Material Library serves as the central case study, showcasing blockchain's capacity to:
- Reconcile scalability with institutional collaboration in large-scale digitization efforts
- Provide an NFT-driven economic model for sustainable cultural heritage valorization
- Validate blockchain's core value in reconstructing cultural heritage governance
- Ensure resource authenticity through smart contracts and open ecosystems
- Shift preservation paradigms from passive conservation to active revitalization

The paper projects that emerging blockchain-based cultural asset exchanges will establish a digital rights trading market for cultural relics exceeding **1 billion RMB by 2030**, transforming industries along the Silk Road cultural economic belt.

### 5.2. Case Study: Salsal Platform

The Salsal case study demonstrates:
- Embedding the ethical guidelines of the International Council of Museums (ICOM) into Ethereum-based smart contracts
- Enabling ethical curation through decentralized governance
- Resolving persistent tensions between transparency and regulatory compliance
- Museums restructured acquisition teams into dual roles: **blockchain auditors** (verifying on-chain provenance) and **ethics compliance officers** (aligning smart contracts with UNESCO regulations)

### 5.3. Cultural Heritage Blockchain Platforms

The paper surveys existing blockchain platforms across three domains:

#### Preservation of Cultural Heritage

| Platform/System | Achievement |
|---|---|
| **Salsal** | Leverages NFTs and smart contracts to authenticate and track cultural artifact collections, addressing ethical and legal challenges in modern curation |
| **Heirloom** | Uses NFTs and IPFS to mitigate risk of heritage extinction/damage due to lack of promotion and funding |
| **MApp** | Integrates blockchain with IPFS for protecting, documenting, and verifying minor artworks; addresses art forgery and information tampering |
| **Zhang et al.** | Blockchain-IoT transport monitoring system tracking environmental variables (temperature, GPS location) ensuring data immutability during transit |

**Key concerns raised**: While decentralization could empower marginalized communities in heritage authentication, technical barriers (coding literacy, infrastructure access) may still exclude them. Blockchain's immutability risks freezing heritage narratives, potentially suppressing dynamic or contested interpretations.

#### Copyright Management

| Platform/System | Achievement |
|---|---|
| **BMDRM** | Replaces centralized authorization with NFT-driven smart contracts; eliminates opacity and tampering risks in transaction records |
| **Bitcoin blockchain timestamping** | Provides immutable timestamping for digital content; enables audit trails to detect unauthorized alterations |

**Key concerns raised**: NFT-based commodification of indigenous heritage risks reducing cultural value to transactional logic, conflicting with communal ownership norms.

#### Museum Applications

| Platform/System | Achievement |
|---|---|
| **MAXP** | Establishes secure digital artifact exchange platforms; mitigates cybersecurity risks through decentralized verification |
| **Traceable one-time address scheme** | Embeds privacy-preserving mechanisms into digital asset interactions; permits regulatory oversight without compromising user privacy |
| **BIM + IoT + Blockchain** | Creates smart museum ecosystems for real-time exhibit monitoring and visitor engagement |

### 5.4. Infrastructure Enhancement and Challenges

#### System Efficiency and Scalability

- **IPFS Integration**: Offloading large metadata to IPFS mitigates on-chain storage overhead while retaining cryptographic verifiability. Heirloom's adoption yielded measurable improvements in transaction efficiency.
- **Role-Based Authorization**: Yun-Ciao Wang's multi-stakeholder framework (museum, licensee, proxy) streamlines rights verification workflows through smart contract automation.
- **Organizational Impact**: IT teams must adopt hybrid skills in blockchain-node management and IPFS metadata governance, merging curatorial and technical roles.

#### Cross-Chain Technology and Interoperability

- **Hyperledger Fabric Consortiums**: MAXP's implementation of Fabric's private channels for cross-border artifact exchanges enhanced operational synchronization.
- **ERC-1155 Token Standard**: MApp unified NFT-based artifact records (provenance, restoration) across Ethereum and Polygon chains, enhancing traceability for 15,000+ minor artworks.
- **Workflow Shift**: Museums now require "heritage data stewards" to oversee multi-chain metadata schemas.

#### Regulation and Standardization

- **Decentralized Governance**: Salsal's blockchain review system prompted museums to restructure into blockchain auditors and ethics compliance officers. Liang et al.'s traceable management system uses smart contracts for dynamic access control lists, streamlining cross-border collaboration.
- **Role Evolution**: Curators transitioned into co-designers of smart contracts with ethical sourcing frameworks. Registrars evolved into blockchain-authenticated validators, certifying metadata provenance through cryptographic verification. HeriLedger replaced physical loan documentation with smart-contract-executed agreements.
- **Operational Metrics**: Integration of decentralized storage with BIM systems optimized environmental monitoring expenditures. GDPR-compliant cryptographic frameworks achieved donor data anonymization while maintaining secured audit trails.

## 6. Challenges and Solutions

### 6.1. Persistent Systemic Barriers

The paper identifies two persistent systemic barriers:

1. **Institutional competency gaps**: Technical literacy limitations among heritage custodians, requiring structured training initiatives (e.g., HeriLedger's certification programs).
2. **Regulatory asymmetries**: Legal frameworks remain misaligned with technological capabilities, necessitating adaptive governance models (e.g., MAXP's cross-domain compliance testing platforms).

### 6.2. Challenges Synthesis

| Challenge Domain | Specific Issues |
|---|---|
| **Scalability** | Blockchain's redundancy ensures data integrity but imposes latency and storage costs |
| **Interoperability** | Fragmented heritage databases hinder global collaboration |
| **Privacy vs. Transparency** | Immutable records conflict with sensitive information requirements |
| **Regulatory Compliance** | Misalignment between decentralized technology and jurisdictional legal frameworks |
| **Energy Consumption** | Especially for PoW-based systems |
| **Technical Barriers** | Coding literacy and infrastructure access may exclude marginalized communities |
| **Ethical Concerns** | Immutability may freeze heritage narratives; NFT commodification may conflict with communal ownership |
| **Institutional Readiness** | Heritage custodians require new technical competencies |

## 7. Future Trends and Research Directions

### 7.1. Technological Innovation

1. **Context-aware unique identifiers**: Blockchain identifiers should extend beyond static metadata (provenance, ownership) to integrate IoT and incorporate additional conservation metrics such as environmental conditions and restoration histories. Embedding sensor-generated contextual data into blockchain records enables adaptive conservation strategies.

2. **AI-based watermarking systems**: Using advanced cryptography such as zero-knowledge proofs (ZKP) to protect copyrights while maintaining data usability. These innovations aim to address scalability and security limitations of existing systems.

3. **Enhanced Raft algorithm**: The proposed enhanced Raft algorithm and context-aware unique identifier mechanism are positioned to expand blockchain's applicability in DCHR management by optimizing consensus efficiency and enabling dynamic identifier tracking.

### 7.2. Application Extension

1. **Decentralized Autonomous Organizations (DAOs)**: Optimizing cross-border artifact loans by automating multi-stakeholder consensus via smart contracts.

2. **Programmable NFTs**: Incorporating usage-rights clauses (exhibition limits, reproduction terms) to enforce ethical access controls. BMDRM's implementation highlights NFT-based licensing potential.

3. **Blockchain + Augmented Reality (AR)**: Establishing immersive provenance verification systems allowing public access to artifact histories through immutable ledger entries.

4. **Positioning blockchain as participatory infrastructure**: For global heritage stewardship, transcending traditional archival roles.

### 7.3. Policy and Regulatory Support

1. **International regulatory sandboxes**: Led by organizations like UNESCO, testing hybrid frameworks that reconcile data privacy regulations across jurisdictions (e.g., GDPR and China's PIPL).

2. **Mandatory blockchain provenance standards**: In national heritage laws, enhancing traceability for high-risk artifacts (including conflict antiquities) by enforcing metadata consistency.

3. **Carbon-neutral consensus protocols**: Aligned with global climate goals, incentivizing transitions to energy-efficient mechanisms. China's blockchain certification system exemplifies harmonizing technical rigor with ethical imperatives.

## 8. Discussion

### 8.1. Key Findings

The paper validates blockchain's transformative potential through two pivotal case studies:

- **Digital Dunhuang**: Demonstrates blockchain's capacity to reconcile scalability with institutional collaboration, providing an NFT-driven economic model for sustainable heritage valorization.
- **Salsal**: Demonstrates embedding ICOM ethical guidelines into Ethereum smart contracts, enabling ethical curation through decentralized governance.

### 8.2. Socio-Political Implications

Drawing on Smith's **Authorized Heritage Discourse (AHD)** framework, the paper identifies a dual impact:
- Blockchain's decentralized nature potentially **challenges** traditional heritage discourse monopolized by professional institutions and governments.
- However, blockchain's technical architecture may establish a new **"technocratic authority"**, transferring discursive power to developer communities through coding paradigms while potentially ossifying singular interpretations of digital heritage via algorithmic determinism.

This represents a thoughtful critical observation about the unintended consequences of applying decentralized technology to inherently contested cultural domains.

## Overall Assessment

### Strengths

1. **Novel application domain**: The paper addresses an underexplored intersection of blockchain technology and cultural heritage preservation, offering a fresh perspective distinct from the more common blockchain-IoT or blockchain-finance literature.

2. **Comprehensive blockchain primer**: Provides an accessible yet thorough overview of blockchain evolution, core features, classification, and consensus mechanisms, making it useful as a reference even for readers unfamiliar with blockchain fundamentals.

3. **Detailed consensus mechanism analysis for DCHR**: The paper goes beyond generic descriptions to systematically evaluate each consensus mechanism's specific suitability for DCHR applications, which is a valuable and original contribution.

4. **Strong case studies**: The Digital Dunhuang Open Material Library and Salsal platform case studies provide concrete, real-world grounding for the theoretical arguments.

5. **Multidimensional impact framework**: The paper articulates impacts across economic, technological, policymaking, and educational dimensions, demonstrating a broad vision for blockchain's role in cultural heritage.

6. **Critical ethical awareness**: The paper does not uncritically advocate for blockchain adoption. It raises important concerns about technical barriers excluding marginalized communities, immutability freezing heritage narratives, and NFT commodification conflicting with communal ownership norms.

7. **Practical proposals**: The enhanced Raft algorithm and context-aware unique identifier mechanism represent concrete technical contributions beyond a pure survey.

8. **Engagement with social theory**: The reference to Smith's Authorized Heritage Discourse (AHD) framework and analysis of blockchain's "technocratic authority" demonstrates interdisciplinary depth.

### Weaknesses

1. **Limited systematic methodology**: Unlike formal systematic reviews, the paper does not describe a reproducible search strategy, inclusion/exclusion criteria, or quality assessment of cited studies. It is unclear how the 86 references were selected and whether the review is comprehensive or selective.

2. **Narrow geographic and linguistic scope**: The paper is heavily weighted toward Chinese case studies and Chinese blockchain industry data. While this reflects the authors' context, it limits generalizability. Western and Global South perspectives on heritage governance are underrepresented.

3. **Insufficient quantitative evidence**: The paper makes claims about efficiency improvements (e.g., "measurable improvements in transaction efficiency," "significantly improved system security") without providing specific quantitative data, metrics, or benchmarks. The projected "1 billion RMB by 2030" market size lacks a cited source.

4. **Incomplete coverage of existing literature**: Important blockchain-heritage projects outside China (e.g., European Union Digital Cultural Heritage initiatives, the British Museum's blockchain experiments) are not discussed. The citation base is relatively small (86 references for a review paper in this broad domain).

5. **Consensus mechanism analysis is theoretical**: Despite the detailed analysis of consensus mechanisms, no empirical comparison is provided (e.g., latency benchmarks, throughput measurements, energy consumption data for DCHR-specific deployments).

6. **Enhanced Raft algorithm insufficiently detailed**: The proposed enhanced Raft algorithm is mentioned as a key contribution but is not formally specified. There are no pseudocode, performance evaluations, or comparative benchmarks against standard Raft or other consensus mechanisms.

7. **Weak challenge-solution mapping**: While challenges and solutions are discussed, there is no systematic framework mapping specific challenges to specific solutions with evidence of effectiveness.

8. **Scalability concerns not adequately addressed**: The paper acknowledges scalability as a key challenge but does not deeply explore solutions such as Layer 2 scaling, state channels, or rollups that are prevalent in the broader blockchain literature.

9. **Limited discussion of failure cases**: The paper presents primarily positive case studies without analyzing failures, abandoned projects, or unsuccessful blockchain-heritage implementations that could provide equally valuable lessons.

10. **Writing quality issues**: Some passages contain repetitive phrasing and overly complex sentence structures. The paper would benefit from more concise expression in several sections.

### Structural Comments

The paper follows a logical structure: Introduction → Blockchain Overview → DCHR Definition and Challenges → Consensus Mechanisms → Implementation → Challenges and Solutions → Future Directions → Discussion. However, the Blockchain Overview section (covering evolution, features, classification, and consensus mechanisms) occupies a disproportionate fraction of the paper (~5 of 11 pages), leaving relatively less space for the more novel contributions (implementation analysis, case studies, and future directions).

### Significance and Relevance

The paper makes a meaningful contribution by bridging blockchain technology and cultural heritage preservation, a domain where trust, provenance, and authenticity are paramount concerns. It is particularly relevant for:

- Researchers exploring non-financial applications of blockchain
- Cultural heritage professionals evaluating digital preservation strategies
- Policymakers designing governance frameworks for digital cultural assets
- Technologists developing blockchain solutions for institutional environments

The engagement with critical perspectives (AHD framework, ethical concerns about tokenization, community exclusion risks) elevates the paper beyond a purely technical survey. However, the lack of methodological rigor in the review process and insufficient empirical evidence for the proposed solutions limit its impact as a definitive reference in this domain.
